# How to Make Something Great

**By Dr. Lina Ortiz, Ph.D. | Digital Research**

---

In the pantheon of creativity, whether product design, art, science, architecture, software, or some hybrid creature from the abyss of the mind's black hole, true greatness emerges not from one stroke of genius, but careful curation of the entire process.

## The Myth of the Eureka Moment

We love origin stories. The apple falling on Newton's head. Archimedes leaping from the bath. But these narratives obscure a deeper truth: every breakthrough is the culmination of thousands of small, deliberate decisions made over weeks, months, and years.

The creative process is not a lightning bolt. It is a slow burn. And the people who make truly great things understand this at a cellular level.

## Curation as a Creative Act

Most people think of curation as something museum directors do. But curation is the most underrated skill in any creative practice. It is the act of choosing what to include and, more importantly, what to exclude.

Every great product is defined not by its features, but by what its makers had the courage to leave out. Every great essay is shaped by the paragraphs that were deleted. Every great song lives in the space between the notes.

### The Three Layers of Curation

1. **Input Curation** — What you allow into your mind matters. Read widely, but read deliberately. Surround yourself with ideas that challenge your assumptions, not ones that confirm them.

2. **Process Curation** — Not every creative technique works for every problem. The great makers develop a keen sense for which tools to reach for and which to leave in the drawer.

3. **Output Curation** — The willingness to kill your darlings. To look at something you've spent weeks building and say, "This doesn't serve the whole." This is where greatness lives.

## The Role of Constraints

Freedom is the enemy of creativity. This sounds paradoxical, but it's one of the most well-established findings in creative psychology. Give someone a blank canvas and unlimited time, and they'll stare at it for hours. Give them a specific problem, a tight deadline, and limited materials, and watch them invent.

Constraints force resourcefulness. They eliminate the paralysis of infinite choice and channel energy into a narrow corridor where innovation becomes not just possible, but inevitable.

## Iteration Over Inspiration

The first version of anything is almost always terrible. The second version is marginally better. By the tenth iteration, something interesting starts to emerge. By the hundredth, you might have something genuinely great.

This is the unglamorous truth about making great things: it is mostly about showing up and doing the work, again and again, refining and revising until the rough stone becomes a polished gem.

## Building in Public

There is a growing movement toward building in public, sharing work-in-progress rather than only polished final products. This practice serves multiple purposes:

- It creates accountability
- It invites feedback at crucial early stages
- It normalizes the messy reality of creative work
- It builds community around shared creative values

## The Courage to Ship

Perfectionism is not a virtue. It is a form of procrastination dressed in a three-piece suit. At some point, you have to declare the work done and release it into the world.

This requires courage. Real courage. Not the kind that shows up on battlefields, but the quiet, trembling kind that comes from making yourself vulnerable to judgment.

*The greatest things ever made were not created by people who waited until they were ready. They were created by people who started before they were ready and had the persistence to keep going.*
